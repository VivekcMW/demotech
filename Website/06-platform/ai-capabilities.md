# AI that saves clinical time — with the doctor always in command

We use AI where it removes drudgery and surfaces information, never where it replaces clinical judgement. Every AI output in {Product} is a draft or a flag — a human signs everything that matters.

---

## Where AI works in the product

**Voice-to-structured-note.** Dictate the consultation in Indian-accented English (mixed regional-language phrases handled); the system produces a structured SOAP note — complaints, findings, diagnosis, plan — for the doctor to review and sign. Typical OPD documentation time drops dramatically; the signature, and the responsibility, stay human.

**Discharge summary drafting.** The summary assembles from what actually happened — diagnoses, course, procedures, investigations with trends, medications on discharge — as a draft for the treating doctor to edit and sign. The end of retype-from-the-file.

**Coding assistance.** Suggested ICD-10 codes from the clinical narrative, ranked with rationale, accepted or corrected by humans. Cleaner coding means cleaner claims and honest analytics.

**Clinical decision support.** Drug–drug interactions, allergy cross-checks, dose-range checks (paediatric and renal), duplicate-order detection, and early-warning scores from charted vitals. Tuned against alert fatigue: severity-tiered, so interruptions are rare and meaningful.

**Operational intelligence.** Discharge-likelihood views for bed planning, no-show risk for scheduling, stock-out prediction for pharmacy — assistive forecasts, clearly labelled as such.

---

## The safety rails, stated plainly

- AI never auto-signs, auto-prescribes, or auto-finalises anything clinical
- Every AI-assisted artefact is marked as AI-drafted in the audit trail until human-signed
- Patient data used for AI features is processed under the same DPDP-grade controls as everything else — and is not used to train models for other customers without explicit agreement
- Features degrade gracefully: if AI services are unreachable, the manual workflow is always present

We'd rather under-promise here. Hospitals run on trust, and so does this page.
