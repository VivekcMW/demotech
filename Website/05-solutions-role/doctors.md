## A day with {Product}, as a consultant

**9:00 am, OPD.** Your list is on screen with each patient's last visit summary one glance away. The consultation note is your specialty's template — not a generic form — with your own quick-phrases and normal-findings defaults. Prescription: type three letters of the drug, pick from your favourites, the interaction check runs silently and only interrupts when it matters. Print and WhatsApp in the patient's language. Next patient.

**1:30 pm, wards.** Rounds on your phone or the ward tablet: vitals trends, overnight nursing notes, this morning's labs with abnormal flags. Progress note by voice. New orders in two taps from your own order sets.

**6:00 pm, anywhere.** The critical-value alert reaches you directly, with the trend, not just the number. You adjust the order remotely. The discharge summary for tomorrow's planned discharge has assembled itself from the record — you edit and sign, you don't retype.

## What changes for you

**Your time comes back.** Documentation that took 2–3 hours of a clinic day compresses to minutes-per-patient. The system types; you decide.

**Your patients are safer on your watch.** Allergy banners that can't be hidden, interaction and dose checks tuned to interrupt rarely and matter when they do, and abnormal results that escalate until acknowledged — designed clinically, not bureaucratically.

**Your clinical record becomes an asset.** Outcomes, follow-up compliance, and your own case mix become queryable — for audits, papers, conference data, and the simple question "how did my last 50 cases of X do?"

**Your prescriptions are NMC-clean by default.** Generic names included, legible always, formatted to the regulations without you thinking about it.

## What doctors ask us

**Q: I've used an EMR before and it doubled my consultation time. Why is this different?**
A: Because the unit of design is your specialty's actual consultation, not a generic encounter form. Measure us in the demo: bring your three commonest cases and time the documentation against your current method.

**Q: Can I dictate in my own mix of English and my language?**
A: Yes — clinical dictation handles Indian-accented English mixed with regional-language phrases; drug names and doses are kept in standard form for safety.

**Q: Who owns my templates and favourites if I move hospitals?**
A: Your personal templates and favourites export with you. The hospital owns the patient records; you keep your way of working.
